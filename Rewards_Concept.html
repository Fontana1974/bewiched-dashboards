STAR

Bewiched Rewards

A tiered rewards programme built to protect Bewiched's margin while making customers feel more rewarded, not less. Earn Stars, climb the metal tiers, and unlock rewards that cost us pennies but feel like pounds — instead of cash-off, which is the single most expensive thing we can give away.

The problem in one line

Cash-off rewards — £1 / £3 / £5 Off — dominate the redemption mix, and they are the most margin-destructive reward we can possibly give. A £1-off costs us a full £1 of gross profit. A free latte feels like £3.40 to the customer but costs us roughly 42p in milk, beans and cup. We are currently giving away the expensive thing and under-using the cheap-but-valuable one. That's backwards.

The core move: stop giving cash, start giving coffee

Every reward sits somewhere on the gap between what it costs us and what it feels worth to the customer. The whole strategy is to push redemptions into the top-right of that gap — high perceived value, near-zero cost to us — and tier the access so it drives behaviour.

The Stars trick. Stars are deliberately generous-feeling — 5 per £1 means a £3 coffee earns 15 Stars, and a free coffee lands at ~150 Stars. The customer feels showered in Stars; we're only ever redeeming them against an item that costs us pennies. Big numbers, small cost.

Margin: before vs after

Same volume of reward "value" handed to customers (~£34.4k QTD), modelled under today's cash-heavy mix vs the concept's coffee-led mix. The customer feeling stays the same or improves; the real cost to us roughly halves and then some. This holds whether we run three tiers or four — the saving comes from the reward mix, not the number of tiers.

Today — cash-led mix

~75% of reward value is cash-off (~£25.8k, lost pound-for-pound) + ~25% free drinks (~£1.1k real cost). Real GP cost ≈ £26.9k QTD for £34.4k of perceived value — 78p in the pound.

Concept — coffee-led mix

Flip to ~20% cash-off (capped) + ~80% high-margin items. Real GP cost ≈ £10.9k QTD for the same £34.4k of perceived value — 32p in the pound. Indicative protection ~£16k/quarter (~£64k/yr), before breakage and frequency upside.

The behavioural engine ("illusion of value")

The levers that make a low-cost scheme feel generous, and quietly work in our favour.

★

Generous-feeling Stars

5 Stars per £1 and big redemption numbers make the scheme feel rich — while every redemption lands on an item costing us pennies.

⏳

Breakage

Stars expire after 6 months of inactivity; tier status decays on the rolling window. Industry breakage runs 10–25% of issued value — reward never claimed is margin retained.

🎯

Aspirational thresholds

The top tier is visible to all but reached by ~3–4%. Most aspire and lift their spend chasing it; few fully exploit the perks. The gap is the win.

✨

Surprise & delight

Random "Gold Star" free upgrades and double-Star weekends — cheap, memorable, and timed to fill trough trading periods.

⚡

Status perks that cost ~£0

Skip-the-queue order-ahead, early access to launches, name on the in-store screen. Zero COGS, high felt status — and they pull customers into store.

🤝

Attach, not discount

Treat-pairings (cookie + coffee) and bring-a-friend rewards generate incremental sales rather than discounting an existing one — reward spend that grows revenue.

What I need you to weigh in on

This is a concept to react to. The structure works on the data, but these calls are genuinely yours to make.

1 · Three tiers or four? (Platinum in or out)
Bronze / Silver / Gold is the simple, recommended core. Platinum adds a rare aspirational halo above Gold (top ~3–4%) for almost no extra cost — but it's one more tier to explain at the counter. The economics are identical either way.
My lean: your stated preference — three core tiers, with Platinum held in reserve as an easy add-on once the scheme beds in.
2 · Do the metal tier names land?
Bronze → Silver → Gold (→ Platinum) is instantly understood and pairs naturally with the gold Bewiched star and the "Stars" currency. Clean, premium, no explanation needed.
My lean: yes — metals read as status everywhere, and Gold ties straight to the brand star.
3 · Spend-based or visit-based tiers?
Spend-based (proposed) rewards basket size and protects ATV. Visit-based (P75 = 6, P90 = 13, P95 = 21 visits / 90 days) would drive raw frequency harder — better for the lfl line, but a £2.50 daily-coffee customer climbs as fast as a £6 one.
My lean: spend-based primary, with a visit-count "fast-track" to the next tier as a secondary trigger.
4 · Do we keep cash-off at all?
Keeping a small, capped cash-off (e.g. lapsed-customer win-back only) is a useful blunt instrument. But every pound of it is a pound of GP. The concept caps it at ~20% of reward value and falling.
My lean: keep it only as a win-back lever, never a default earn.
5 · Rolling window length — 90 days?
90 days matches our data window and feels fair. 30 days drives frequency harder but risks demoting good customers after one quiet fortnight (holidays, illness) and feeling punitive.
My lean: 90 days, reviewed monthly so status updates feel live, not cliff-edge.
Caveat on the numbers. COGS and perceived-value figures are working estimates for the concept and the margin model is illustrative — it holds perceived reward value constant and varies only the mix. Before any launch we'd lock COGS against real recipe costings and model the actual current redemption split (£1 / £3 / £5 / free-drink) rather than the assumed 75/25. Economics are unchanged from the prior concept — this revision re-themes the tiers to Bronze/Silver/Gold(+Platinum) and sets the genuine Bewiched star as the hero.
